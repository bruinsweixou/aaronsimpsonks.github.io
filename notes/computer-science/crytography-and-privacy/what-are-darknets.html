What is a Darknet?

What is a darknet? According to Wikipedia, a darknet is an “overlay network within the Internet that can only be accessed with specific software, configurations, or authorization, and often uses a unique customized communication protocol."

An overlay network is simply any network that runs atop - or is overlayed - on another network.

The most famous example of this is actually the Internet itself. In its early days, the easiest way to construct a computing network was to take advantage of the existing telephone infrastructure, hence things like dial-up that killed your connection if your mom wanted to call your aunt. An advantage of overlay networks is the abstraction of the underlying network architecture, meaning that what is a multi-hop path in the underlying network can be treated as a single link between two nodes in the overlaying network.

Specific software could be tools like Tor or a peer-to-peer filesharing application like Napster. Authorization would be things like password protection or cryptographic keys, ensuring that only specific trusted individuals could access the network. Communication protocols specify how information being sent from one member of a network to another should be formatted, as well as what information a valid message needs to include, such as an IP address to identify the sender and destination, a payload, or a connection timeout date.

Despite the name “darknet”, nothing about this concept is inherently malicious or implies illegality. There’s nothing illegal about private networks, nor should there be. Indeed, despite the illegal activities they are best known for, applications like Tor have been valuable tools for those living under authoritarian regimes.
