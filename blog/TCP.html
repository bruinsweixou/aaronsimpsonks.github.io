Transmission Control Protocol (TCP)

July 18th, 2021

Our modern world is interconnected. We speak through social media, send emails, watch Netflix, etc. We also speak in person through language, customs, and culture. All these things - emails, language, and the internet itself - rely on protocols. A protocol is system that two or more parties agree upon for purposes of communication and the transmission of information. By following a protocol, communication becomes almost transactional; so long as I provide you the required pieces of information i.e. satisfy my end of the protocol, you should be able to provide me something in return.

Language itself is a form of protocol. For English speakers, we have an alphabet of characters and punctuation, as well as grammatical rules that we use to combine them in meaningful ways. So long as you and I both understand this alphabet and set of rules, we should be able to communicate with one another, rather than just screeching and making random noises.

While devices connected to the internet don't speak English necessarily, they rely on the exact same principle to function. The same way that the rules of English allow you to read the words on this page right now, protocols are what make Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, Netflix, email, and other things all work seamlessly. In fact, if we're talking about the internet, one protocol in particular is important - the Transmission Control Protocol or TCP.
