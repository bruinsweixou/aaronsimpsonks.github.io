Teaching Myself CS

July 6th, 2021

I'm teaching myself computer science...again...kind of.

Apart from work, I haven't done much programming since graduating college. A part of me has hated myself for it, dying to pop open the text editor and start cracking away. The rest reassures me that I've been through a lot and taking some time off is healthy. I can now confidently admit that the last two years of my degree burned me out in ways I'm still fully processing, so the latter half is probably on to something.

I never wanted to be just a software engineer; I wanted to be the best software engineer I could possibly be. Even though I did well in my university courses like Data Structures & Algorithms, I never quite grasped the material as well as I'd have liked to. I think anyone who's been through college can attest that the optimization for a grade sometimes comes at the cost of truly understanding the subject matter.

As an engineer, I naturally approach things from a first-order perspective - I like building strong foundations and introducing new knowledge as circumstances necessitate, rather than having everything thrown at me all at once or trying to work backwards from the top. For example, the best way to teach recursion in my opinion is to start with a problem that requires it and introduce it as a solution, rather than starting with "teaching recursion" and backwards-rationalizing problems to further that end. I want to return to the roots and relearn computer science at a pace I'm comfortable with, starting all the way back at the first "Hello, World" and going from there. As part of this goal, I'm sharing some resources I found as a sort of "curriculum" to help guide my learning.

Teach Yourself Computer Science

Link

This resource seems to be exactly what I'm looking for - a place for self-taught engineers seeking to broaden their foundations and learn skills like programming, data structures, compilers, and more. I'm familiar enough to know that this curriculum has a nice even mix of brevity and depth. Understanding everything here at even a base level would put me above and beyond what most engineers deal with in their day-to-day.
